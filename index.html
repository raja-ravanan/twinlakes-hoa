Pet Owners · Important Reminder

Leash Law & Responsible Pet Ownership

Jefferson County Code Section 91.002 requires that all animals (except community cats) be kept under restraint at all times. It is illegal to walk your dog off-leash, except in designated dog parks.

Louisville Metro Animal Services enforces this law and can be reached 24 hours a day at 502-333-9072.

ViolationSection 91.002 is a Class B misdemeanor: fine of $5–$250 and 5–90 days imprisonment, or both. A civil penalty of $150–$1,000 may also be issued.
LicensingAll dogs and cats over 4 months old must be licensed.
Dog BitesOwners must report a dog bite within 24 hours. Failure to report carries a $100–$500 fine.
EnforcementAn animal that has bitten someone can be picked up and quarantined; the law provides for euthanizing vicious animals. Over 6,000 such incidents occurred in Louisville Metro last year.

Please keep pets leashed, license your pets, and report bites within 24 hours. Thank you for helping us create a safe and welcoming community! Questions? Contact Louisville Metro Animal Services at 502-333-9072.

Pond Maintenance · July 2026

Pond Maintenance Update

The board wants to keep our community informed on the current status of the Twin Lakes ponds and the steps being taken to address recent concerns.

July 2026 Update — Vendor Selected & Treatment Underway

The board worked with three different pond-management vendors to understand the root cause and underlying issues with the small pond, with a focus on putting a sound long-term solution in place. After a thorough analysis, the board selected Jones Management. Jones has begun their work — the first application was completed on July 9, 2026. More details will be shared shortly.

Background: The Smaller Pond

The smaller pond has experienced increased aquatic vegetation growth this season. After bringing in multiple independent vendors to assess the situation, here is what we learned:

  • The pond is shallow (under 6 feet), which makes it prone to chronic vegetation and algae growth
  • Lawn fertilizer runoff carries nitrogen and phosphorus into the pond, which fuels that growth
  • This condition existed in prior years but was masked by blue dye treatments used by our previous vendor — it did not develop overnight
  • Plants identified include pond weed and creeping water primrose

This diagnosis guided the board’s vendor selection and the treatment now underway (see the July update above).

The Two Larger Ponds

The larger ponds are in better condition due to their greater depth. As a precaution, vendors identified early signs of invasive aquatic plant growth (alligator weed) and elevated nutrient levels that could lead to algae issues if left unaddressed. We are taking proactive steps to treat these before they become a visible problem.

Monday, July 14, 2026

Special Community Meeting

Thank you to everyone who attended the Special Community Meeting. The Board appreciated the strong homeowner turnout and the thoughtful questions and feedback shared throughout the evening. The purpose of the meeting was to provide updates on several important Association matters, answer homeowner questions, and discuss ongoing initiatives. This is a high-level summary of the topics discussed — it is intended to keep homeowners informed and is not the official Board meeting minutes or a verbatim transcript.

Pond Management

Pond management was the primary topic of the evening. The Board reviewed the history of pond maintenance since the Association transitioned from developer to homeowner control, and explained how vendors were selected over the past year. Earlier this year the Board selected a lower-cost vendor after reviewing multiple proposals and references, balancing budget limits against service expectations. As pond conditions deteriorated and that vendor became increasingly unresponsive, the Board terminated the contract and sought new proposals. After onsite evaluations, the Board selected Jones Lake Management for the remainder of the season. Representatives from Jones attended and presented on the differences between the small pond and the two larger lakes, the causes of current conditions, their treatment strategy, and the expected timeline.
  • The small pond needs significantly more intensive treatment due to its shallow depth and stormwater runoff; treatments will continue roughly every two weeks.
  • The two larger lakes remain in generally good condition and currently need only limited maintenance.
  • Visible improvement is expected over the next 6–8 weeks, with continued improvement through consistent maintenance.
  • Long-term options such as nutrient reduction, aeration, and other capital projects may be considered in future years as funding allows.
  • The Board will continue working with vendors to reduce nutrient runoff from landscaping around the ponds where practical.

Financial Update

The Treasurer presented an independently prepared financial analysis based on invoices, bank statements, and other supporting information. Several homeowners noted that it differed from the monthly financial reports prepared by Mulloy Properties.
Board Clarification
  • The independently prepared analysis had not been presented to or reviewed by the Board before the meeting, so the Board was not in a position to comment on the reported differences during the meeting.
  • The Board conducts its financial oversight using the monthly reports prepared by Mulloy Properties, the Association’s professional management company responsible for its financial records.
  • Board decisions on budgets, expenditures, and reserve funding have been based on those official management reports.

The Board confirmed that Mulloy’s official reports remain the records used for oversight, and agreed the Treasurer’s analysis would be reviewed against them to identify and explain any differences.

The Board remains committed to responsible financial oversight, transparency, and providing homeowners with accurate and consistent financial information.

HOA Financial Status

The Board gave an overview of the Association’s financial position through June, covering operating budgets (budgeted vs. actual), reserve funding, snow removal, insurance costs, irrigation maintenance, and utility expenses. Several categories exceeded original projections because of an unusually severe winter, insurance requirements following the transition from the developer, and infrastructure-related maintenance. The Board continues to closely monitor expenditures and reserve balances.
Mid-Year Snapshot

Even with a few categories running higher than projected, through mid-year the HOA and Garden Homes are approximately $13,000 under budget combined and have built approximately $5,000 in reserves across the two reports.

HOA Tax ID Structure

Twin Lakes was established as a single legal entity by the developer. Homeowners asked whether Garden Homes and Patriot Homes should operate under separate tax identification numbers. The Board explained the benefits of remaining a single entity — lower administrative costs, simplified accounting, reserve tax advantages, a single insurance structure, and shared responsibility for common areas and amenities — and noted that creating separate entities would introduce additional legal, financial, accounting, insurance, and governance complexity.

HOA Elections

The Board shared a preliminary roadmap for the upcoming Board election:
  • Early Fall: election notification distributed by Mulloy Properties.
  • September: candidate nominations.
  • October: in-person election.
  • January 1, 2027: new Board terms begin.
Homeowners expressed support for the idea of staggered Board terms, and raised suggestions on continuity between Boards and committee participation. These ideas will be reviewed as part of future governance discussions, with additional election details to follow before the nomination period.

HOA Website

The Board introduced the newly launched HOA website, www.TwinLakesLouisville.com, developed to improve communication and give homeowners convenient access to community information — governing documents, Board information, the vendor directory, community announcements, meeting information, and online homeowner requests. The website will serve as the central hub for community information going forward, with more content and features being added.

Landscaping & Community Maintenance

Residents shared concerns about several aspects of community upkeep, including common-area landscaping and weed control, irrigation and street lighting, and tree replacement and maintenance consistency. The Board acknowledged these concerns and will continue working with Association contractors to review reported issues and improve maintenance where appropriate.

Communication

The Board discussed ongoing efforts to improve homeowner communication, including community newsletters, Board meetings, updates distributed through Mulloy Properties, and the HOA website as the central location for community information.

Looking Ahead

The Board will continue focusing on these priorities in the months ahead:
  • Monitoring pond restoration efforts.
  • Maintaining responsible financial oversight.
  • Preparing for the 2027 Board election.
  • Continuing reserve planning.
  • Reviewing landscaping and maintenance concerns.
  • Expanding homeowner resources through the HOA website.
  • Improving communication with residents.
The Board thanked residents for their participation, questions, and feedback, and encouraged everyone to stay engaged. For additional updates, governing documents, meeting information, and homeowner resources, visit www.TwinLakesLouisville.com.

Tuesday, March 10, 2026

First Annual Community Meeting

In attendance: 42 households in person, plus Zoom attendees. Board: Tony Backert (President), Yashu Basavaraju (Vice President), Ramana N (Treasurer), Raja Ravanan (Secretary), Aimee Green, Mike Schnell. Management: Eddie Douglas, Mulloy Properties.

President Tony Backert welcomed residents to the community’s first true HOA annual meeting. Although the homeowner Board was elected in fall 2025, it did not formally take control until January 1, 2026, when the developer (Elite) signed the association over; the Board had worked with Mulloy Properties since October and has met every two weeks since.

Role & Purpose of the HOA

The Board maintains the community and protects property values, manages the vendors who provide those services, reviews and clarifies policies (CCRs and ARC guidelines), and acts as a responsible steward of homeowner dues — noting the developer’s “open checkbook” is gone, so disciplined budgeting now matters.

What the Board Has Accomplished

  • Met every two weeks since being elected, reviewing all vendor contracts.
  • EcoTech (trash & recycling): replaced Republic — which sought substantial increases — at a rate below last year’s, fixed for three years (5% cap in years 4–5). This is why residents saw new carts delivered.
  • TrueGreen: contract continued; a $30–40/home rate negotiated for Patriot homes (details to follow via newsletter).
  • Two Fish (ponds): a new local vendor at better pricing; ~20 grass carp to be introduced across the three ponds in April–May, replenished every 3–4 years.
  • Melendez (Garden-Home cutting), Aphix (common areas — cost up for the new Section 4 / retention areas), and Evergreen (irrigation) continue.
  • 14 ARC requests approved to date; snow, lighting, and garbage-corral policies documented and communicated.
  • Launched the Twin Lakes newsletter (named by resident vote) and a resident Facebook group.

2026 Initiatives

  • Work with the developer to complete outstanding items (curb/construction-damage repairs; bond release).
  • Lake bubblers/aeration — under investigation (~$3,500 each; ~2 for the large pond, 1 each for the others), phased over ~3 years to improve water quality and reduce bug-breeding areas.
  • Geese — evaluating a harmless laser deterrent and natural measures; residents asked not to feed the geese (droppings can be hazardous to children and pets).
  • Mosquitoes — spraying continues; bubblers expected to help.
  • Evaluate the delinquent-dues process (targeted for 2027) and plan community events.

Financial Report

The community has two separate budgets by design (services are segregated): the HOA/Master budget (~$133K) and the Garden Homes budget (~$154K) — together roughly $280,000 in annual spending. Both budgets and the presentation slides will be posted online.
  • 2025 (HOA): budgeted to run a deficit (not all homes closed/paying) but ended with a ~$5,800 surplus.
  • 2025 (Garden Homes): ran a ~$13,000 deficit, which the developer covered — turned over debt-free.
  • Reserves: ~$10,000 total budgeted into 2026 reserves, plus the ~$5,800 carryover. The dues increase funds these reserves to avoid special assessments (the management-proposed budget had no cushion — e.g., the ~$4,000 snow budget was already exceeded).
A resident suggested a consolidated statement; the Board explained the two-association structure and committed to posting both budgets online for full transparency.

Communication, Committees & Directory

  • Slides and minutes to be shared with all residents after the meeting.
  • Newsletter moving to four seasonal editions per year; a private resident Facebook group is active; a community website is planned.
  • A neighborhood directory will be built on an opt-in basis (privacy respected).
  • Day-to-day questions continue through Mulloy (Eddie Douglas / Renee); Eddie was commended for his responsiveness.
  • A social/hospitality committee has formed with 4 volunteers.

Resident Q&A (summary)

  • Old Aiken Road debris / blue trailer: Elite’s staging area; to be cleared and gated after construction completes (~mid-summer).
  • Construction damage (curb/concrete near mailboxes): report to Elite at customercare@elitebuilthomes.com.
  • Roads & sidewalks: become the city’s responsibility once bonds are released; report sidewalk issues to your Metro Council office (don’t self-repair — liability).
  • Aiken Road shoulder / safety: direct road-improvement requests to Metro Council.
  • Retention basin / drainage: MSD’s responsibility (sediment, beaver dams, April flooding) — an ongoing concern the Board is pursuing with MSD. Homeowners may clear embankment growth per MSD guidance, with caution re: erosion liability.
  • Package misdelivery: the Facebook group is a good way to coordinate.
The Board thanked residents for attending, reaffirmed full transparency, and encouraged everyone to sign up for the newsletter and Facebook group — and to consider volunteering.

Board Meeting Minutes

Summaries of the Board’s regular working meetings, most recent first.

Between October 2025 and June 2026, your volunteer Board came together 18 times — more than 10 of those between October 2025 and March 2026 — and gave well over 100 hours of personal time reviewing every contract, reconciling the finances, walking the property, and preparing answers to your questions — to get the community onto steady footing for its first year.

We mention this not for recognition, but so the care behind each decision is clear.

From Developer to Neighbors: Our First Few Months

When Twin Lakes transitioned from developer control to a homeowner-run association, your newly elected Board didn’t wait for January 1 to get to work. Here’s what those months of effort produced.

Getting organized (Oct–Nov 2025). The Board elected its officers — Tony Backert (President), Yashu Basavaraju (Vice President), Ramana N (Treasurer), Raja Ravanan (Secretary), with Aimee Green and Mike Schnell — and formed volunteer committees so more neighbors could get involved. The Board also set the 2026 budget, making the deliberate choice to build a modest reserve for the first time in the community’s history.

Saving money from day one (Nov–Dec 2025). Facing a steep trash-hauling increase, the Board negotiated a switch to EcoTech — lower than the prior year’s cost, with a three-year price lock. It began a full review of every vendor contract and established a process so the Board reviews and approves invoices before they’re paid.

Building communication (Dec 2025–Jan 2026). The Board launched the Twin Lakes newsletter (named by resident vote!), set up a private resident Facebook group, and created a shared, secure records system so community documents live in the community’s hands. New snow, lighting, and trash-screening guidelines were documented and shared.

Doing the homework (Jan–Feb 2026). The Treasurer began a line-by-line review of the finances; the Board walked the property with the developer to catalog outstanding items; gathered insurance quotes; and reviewed 14+ architectural requests. New vendors were finalized — Two Fish for pond care (with grass carp arriving this spring) and a negotiated TrueGreen option for Patriot homes.

Bringing it to you (March 2026). All of this led to the community’s first-ever HOA annual meeting on March 10, 2026 — the full minutes are in the Community Meetings section above.
